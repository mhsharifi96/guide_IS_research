An Interactive Guide to IS Research

Understanding the Landscape

Welcome! This guide translates the complex world of Information Systems (IS) research into an interactive explorer. In IS, we study the intricate relationship between people, organizations, and technology. To do this, researchers adopt different "stances" or goals. This application breaks down these stances, from the high-level philosophies (Paradigms) to the specific tools (Approaches), and lets you dynamically compare them.

At the highest level, IS research can be broadly divided into two major categories based on its primary goal:

Behavioral Science

Goal: To seek truth and understand reality.

This approach focuses on developing and verifying theories that explain or predict human or organizational behavior related to technology. It asks: "Why do people use (or not use) this system?" or "How does this IT impact team performance?"

Design Science

Goal: To seek utility and create artifacts.

This approach focuses on building and evaluating innovative IT artifacts (like models, methods, or software) to solve real-world problems. It asks: "How can we build a better system for this specific problem?"

Research Paradigms: The "Why"

This section explores the philosophical foundations, or paradigms, that underpin Behavioral Science research. A paradigm is a set of core beliefs about the nature of reality (ontology) and how we can know it (epistemology). These beliefs fundamentally shape what questions a researcher asks and how they try to answer them. Click each paradigm to learn more.

Positivist Paradigm

  • Ontology (Reality): Assumes a single, objective reality exists "out there." It is orderly and can be measured and known.
  • Epistemology (Knowledge): The researcher is a detached, objective observer. Knowledge is gained by measuring phenomena and testing hypotheses. The goal is to find universal laws and causal relationships.
  • Common Methods: Surveys, Experiments, Statistical Analysis.
  • Example IS Question: "Does the graphical design of an e-commerce website (Variable A) have a direct, measurable effect on user purchase intent (Variable B)?"

Interpretive Paradigm

  • Ontology (Reality): Assumes reality is socially constructed. There is no single objective reality, only multiple, complex realities as perceived by different people.
  • Epistemology (Knowledge): The researcher is involved and subjective. Knowledge is gained by understanding the meanings people assign to their world. The goal is to understand context and lived experience.
  • Common Methods: Case Studies, Ethnography, Interviews.
  • Example IS Question: "How do frontline employees in a hospital *make sense of* and *experience* the implementation of a new patient record system in their daily work?"

Critical Paradigm

  • Ontology (Reality): Assumes reality is shaped by historical, social, political, and economic power structures. These structures create inequality and oppression.
  • Epistemology (Knowledge): The researcher is an activist and change agent. Knowledge is gained by uncovering these hidden structures. The goal is not just to understand reality, but to critique it and promote social change.
  • Common Methods: Action Research, Critical Discourse Analysis.
  • Example IS Question: "How does the design of an algorithm for hiring 'gig economy' workers reinforce existing power imbalances and social inequalities?"

Research Approaches: The "How"

This section details the specific approaches, or methods, researchers use to conduct their studies. These are the "tools" for gathering and analyzing data, and the choice of tool is guided by the paradigm and the research question. Use the filters to explore methods by their category. Click any card to see its strengths and weaknesses.

  • High generalizability (if sampled well)
  • Cost-effective for large samples
  • Good for descriptive and correlational insights

Weaknesses:

  • Lacks contextual depth ("the why")
  • Risk of respondent bias or low response rates

  • Can be difficult or unethical for some IS topics

  • Extremely high contextual depth and realism
  • Good for studying complex social processes
  • Can build rich, new theories (e.g., from Yin)

Weaknesses:

  • Findings are not statistically generalizable
  • Time-consuming and data-intensive

Strengths:

  • High practical relevance; solves real problems
  • Empowers participants (aligned with Critical paradigm)
  • Iterative cycles of action and reflection

Weaknesses:

  • Difficult to maintain research rigor and objectivity
  • Dual roles of researcher and "consultant" can conflict

  • Unmatched depth for understanding culture and "hidden" practices
  • Captures what people *do*, not just what they *say*

Weaknesses:

  • Extremely time-consuming (months or years)
  • Risk of "going native" (losing objectivity)

Strengths:

  • Creates novel, useful solutions (artifacts)
  • Directly addresses "how to" questions
  • High practical relevance and contribution

Weaknesses:

  • Can be difficult to demonstrate theoretical rigor
  • Evaluation of the artifact can be challenging

How do these methods stack up? Select two or three approaches to dynamically compare them on key attributes. This chart helps visualize the trade-offs a researcher makes. For example, methods high in Generalizability (like surveys) are often low in Contextual Depth (like case studies), and vice-versa.

Key References

This guide is based on foundational academic work in Information Systems research. For a much deeper dive, these papers are the standard-bearers for their respective topics and are highly recommended reading for any aspiring IS researcher.

  • Hevner, A. R., March, S. T., Park, J., & Ram, S. (2004). Design science in information systems research. MIS quarterly, 28(1), 75-105. (The foundational paper for modern Design Science).
  • Orlikowski, W. J., & Baroudi, J. J. (1991). Studying information technology in organizations: Research approaches and assumptions. Information systems research, 2(1), 1-28. (A classic paper outlining the three paradigms).
  • Klein, H. K., & Myers, M. D. (1999). A set of principles for conducting and evaluating interpretive field studies in information systems. MIS quarterly, 23(1), 67-93. (A key guide for doing Interpretive research).
  • Yin, R. K. (2003). Case study research: Design and methods. Sage publications. (The "bible" for conducting rigorous case study research).
  • Walsham, G. (1993). Interpreting information systems in organizations. John Wiley & Sons. (A seminal book on the interpretive approach in IS).
